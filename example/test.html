How a neural network uses spinning circles to do math (mod 113)

1 The Big Secret: A Circle IS a Sine Wave

Key Insight: A sine wave is just the shadow of a point moving around a circle. If you watch a dot go around a circle and track its height over time, you get a sine wave. Track its horizontal position? That's a cosine wave. They're the same thing, just viewed differently!

2 The "Embedding Space" — Numbers Become Points on Circles

When the transformer wants to represent a number like a = 37, it doesn't just store "37". Instead, it places a point on multiple circles — one for each "key frequency" it learned.

Think of it like this: the number 37 becomes an angle on each circle: angle = (2π × frequency × 37) / 113

3 Reading Off the Coordinates: cos and sin

Each point on a circle has two coordinates: cos(angle) = horizontal position, sin(angle) = vertical position. These are just numbers between -1 and +1.

So embedding number 37 at frequency k=14 means computing:
• cos(2π × 14 × 37 / 113) = some number
• sin(2π × 14 × 37 / 113) = some other number

4 The Magic Trick: Adding Angles on Circles = Adding Numbers!

Here's where it gets wild. The transformer needs to compute (a + b) mod 113.

On a circle, adding two numbers is the same as adding their angles!
If a=37 is at angle θ₁ and b=89 is at angle θ₂, then (a+b)=126 is at angle θ₁ + θ₂.

And there's a formula for this (the "trig identity"):
cos(θ₁ + θ₂) = cos(θ₁)·cos(θ₂) − sin(θ₁)·sin(θ₂)
sin(θ₁ + θ₂) = sin(θ₁)·cos(θ₂) + cos(θ₁)·sin(θ₂)

The neural network learned these formulas in its weights! 🤯

5 Multiple Circles → Multiple Waves → One Sharp Peak

Each single circle produces a wavy signal with many bumps. But when we add all 5 waves together, something magical happens: all the waves line up (constructive interference) at exactly one point — the correct answer! Everywhere else, they cancel out (destructive interference).

It's like 5 people singing different notes — at one special spot, they all harmonize perfectly. 🎵

6 The Full Picture: Circles → Sine Waves → Answer

Summary — The whole algorithm:
  1. Embed: Turn numbers into points on 5 circles (at 5 special frequencies)
  2. Read coordinates: Get sin/cos values from each circle position
